# Art Direction Report: Aggression Axis Evaluation

## 1. Artifact Threshold
The "aggression" axis remains largely stable until the extreme poles, but starts to exhibit "uncanny valley" and mesh distortions at the ±3.0 marks. 

On the **aggressive pole (positive)**, the face begins to look "broken" at **+3.0**. The primary failure is in the mouth and nasolabial area; the upper lip pulls upward while the corners are driven downward, creating a pinched, triangular distortion at the corners of the mouth that looks more like a mesh error than a human expression. The compression of the chin also creates a shelf-like protrusion that feels disconnected from the underlying jaw structure.

On the **yielding pole (negative)**, the threshold for distortion is reached at **-2.5 to -3.0**. Here, the jaw elongates significantly, leading to a "muzzle" effect where the lower half of the face feels detached from the skull. The skin around the jawline and neck starts to show sharp, non-anatomical angles. The eye widening at -3.0 also reaches a point where the upper eyelid seems to disappear into the brow ridge, creating a hollowed-out look.

## 2. Gradient Quality
The progression is remarkably smooth and monotonic from -2.0 to +2.0. There are no sudden jumps or reversals in the expression's intent. The transition from neutral (0.0) to slight tension is subtle and well-handled.

The **"sweet spot" range is ±1.5 to ±2.0**. In this window, the expressions are highly legible and emotionally resonant without the anatomical stretching seen at the extremes. At +1.5, the aggression is palpable (the "intent to act" is clear); at -1.5, the submission feels genuine and reactive. Beyond 2.0, the model starts to prioritize parameter intensity over anatomical plausibility.

## 3. Recipe Quality
### Aggressive Pole (+ values)
The positive pole currently reads more as "bitter contempt" or "stifled anger" than "active rage/combat." To move it toward a "punch-throwing" aggression, we need more activation in the mid-face. Specifically, **nasal flare** (dilated nostrils) and a slight **lip curl** (exposing more of the upper teeth) would communicate the physiological readiness for combat. Currently, the mouth is too tightly closed; a "battle cry" or "snarl" variant where the jaw is slightly parted but tense would be more evocative of extreme aggression.

### Yielding Pole (- values)
The negative pole effectively captures "submission/retreat" through eye widening and a slackening of the jaw. It reads as a person who has been "socially defeated" or is reacting to a threat they cannot match. To make it more yielding, we could incorporate a slight **head tilt or gaze aversion** (if the model allows for neck/eye offsets), as direct eye contact at -3.0 still feels a bit too "confrontational in its shock."

### Yielding vs. Fatigue
Yielding is successfully distinct from the exhausted pole of fatigue. If I had to describe the difference: **Fatigue is "heavy," while Yielding is "hollow."** 
In fatigue, the face is succumbing to gravity—the eyelids droop, the brows are flat and heavy, and the mouth is slack from depletion. In yielding, the face is "pulling back"—the brows are raised in alarm, the eyes are wide and "empty" (showing more sclera), and the mouth is open in a gasp of surrender rather than a yawn of exhaustion. Yielding is an active emotional retreat, whereas fatigue is a passive physiological collapse.
