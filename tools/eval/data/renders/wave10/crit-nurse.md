## 1. Distress Signals
The clearest distress signals are in `alarm_1.5.png` and `shape_puffy-alarmed.png`. 
- **Startle/Panic:** Wide eyes with visible sclera (whites) above the iris is a universal "fear" signal. In an ER, this usually means a patient who thinks they are dying (panic attack, pulmonary embolism, or internal bleeding).
- **Physical Alteration:** The "puffy" distortion in `shape_puffy-alarmed.png` is an immediate "red flag" for airway involvement (anaphylaxis). Even computer-generated, that level of facial swelling triggers a "code blue" instinct in an experienced nurse.

## 2. Deceptively Calm
`cross_euphoric-exhausted.png` and `smirk_0.3.png` are the most deceptive. 
- The euphoric/exhausted combination looks like a patient who has just been given a heavy dose of Dilaudid—they say they feel "great" but they're one step away from respiratory depression. 
- A subtle smirk can also be a "mask" for patients with a high pain threshold or those who are trying to hide a neurological deficit (like early-stage Parkinson's or a transient ischemic attack).

## 4. Medically "Impossible" Anatomy
- **`full_puffy-soyboi-euphoric-exhausted.png`**: The "puffy" filter doesn't follow normal lymphatic or vascular drainage patterns. It looks like the face is literally melting off the bone, which in a real patient would mean severe necrotizing fasciitis or some horrific industrial chemical burn.
- **`shape_puffy-alarmed.png`**: The bilateral upper-eyelid swelling is so extreme it looks like "Puffy Eye Syndrome" but on steroids. It's medically "wrong" because it doesn't quite match any single disease process perfectly—it's too symmetrical and localized.
