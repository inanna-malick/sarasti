## Introduction: The Kinematics of Urgency

In the pursuit of digital emotive truth, we often find ourselves negotiating with the very bones of the face. In our latest iteration of the Sarasti visualization system, we have enacted a pivotal change in the mapping of the jaw opening parameter ($\psi_2$). Historically, in Variant C, the jaw was enslaved to the valence axis. This mapping was rooted in a psychological model where "sadness" or "defeat" (negative valence) was expressed through a slack-jawed, open-mouthed surrender to gravity. 

However, in Variant D, we have shifted this parameter to the Tension axis. Under this new regime, jaw drop is no longer a signal of misery, but a signal of *activation*—of urgency, of vocalization, of the high-arousal states that demand an open airway. In Variant D, a closed jaw signifies internalizing, whether that be the quietude of contentment or the tight-lipped stoicism of deep despair.

This report compares these two philosophies through the lens of the "Corner Quadrants"—the extreme poles of our 2D emotive space. We will examine how this single structural shift ripples across the face, altering the stories these characters tell and the readability of their internal lives.

## 1. Per-face Character Study: The Inhabitants of the Quadrants

To truly understand these faces, we must treat them not as data points, but as actors. Each quadrant represents a distinct performance, a specific moment in a character's narrative.

### Variant C: The Valence-Linked Jaw

#### 1.1 "Felix" (vc_corner_manic: Tension=+1, Valence=+1)
**The Performance:** Felix is the quintessential high-energy host. His eyes are wide, sparkling with a manic, almost desperate need to please. Because his valence is positive, his jaw remains relatively set. He wears a broad, social smile—the "mask of joy." But the tension is there in the slight arch of the brows and the rigidity of the neck.
**The Story:** Felix is holding it together. He is "on" for the cameras. He is happy, yes, but it is a happiness that costs him something. He is the person who tells you "everything is great!" while his pulse is at 120 bpm. His joy is contained within a civilized frame.

#### 1.2 "Arthur" (vc_corner_panicked: Tension=+1, Valence=-1)
**The Performance:** Arthur is caught in a moment of sharp, intellectualized concern. His brows are knit tight in a V-shape of distress. His mouth is a thin, hard line, turned down at the corners. Because Variant C links the jaw to valence, and Arthur's valence is extremely negative, his jaw *should* be open, but the high tension is fighting it, pulling the lips into a defensive seal.
**The Story:** Arthur is the man who just realized he made a fatal error. He is paralyzed by the realization. His panic is internal; he is "choking" on his fear. It is a quiet, vibrating terror that hasn't yet found its voice.

#### 1.3 "Silas" (vc_corner_content: Tension=-1, Valence=+1)
**The Performance:** Silas is the image of easy peace. His eyes are soft, the lids heavy but not tired. His mouth is a gentle curve of genuine, low-arousal satisfaction. The jaw is relaxed and closed. There is no urgency here, no need to speak or scream.
**The Story:** Silas is watching a sunset after a long, productive day. He is at home in his skin. His joy is deep and slow, like a low-frequency hum. This is the most "stable" face in the Variant C set.

#### 1.4 "Barnaby" (vc_corner_depressed: Tension=-1, Valence=-1)
**The Performance:** Barnaby is exhausted by his own grief. Here, the "Jaw = Negative Valence" mapping is most apparent. His mouth is slightly open, his jaw slack. His eyes are dull, staring at nothing. The skin tone feels sallow, as if the blood has left his face.
**The Story:** Barnaby has given up. This is the face of a man who has been crying for hours and has now reached a state of numb, slack-jawed defeat. He is a shell. The open jaw doesn't read as a scream; it reads as an inability to even keep his mouth shut.

### Variant D: The Tension-Linked Jaw

#### 1.5 "Zippy" (vd_corner_manic: Tension=+1, Valence=+1)
**The Performance:** Zippy is a revelation. Moving the jaw to the Tension axis has unlocked the "Joyful Scream." His mouth is wide open, exposing the teeth and tongue in a massive, ecstatic shout. His eyes are even wider than Felix's, as if he is trying to drink in the entire world.
**The Story:** Zippy just won the lottery. He is at a concert, and his favorite song just started. This is a public, explosive joy. It is uncontainable. By allowing the jaw to drop with tension, we have given this positive quadrant a "volume" that Variant C lacked.

#### 1.6 "Victor" (vd_corner_panicked: Tension=+1, Valence=-1)
**The Performance:** If Arthur was a quiet panic, Victor is a loud one. His jaw is dropped in a classic "gasp of horror." The furrowed brows combined with the open mouth create a much more primal read. The tension is visible in the tendons of the neck and the sharp angle of the jawline.
**The Story:** Victor is witnessing a catastrophe. He is inhaling sharply, perhaps about to scream a warning. This is a panic of action and adrenaline. It is a face that demands attention; you cannot ignore Victor's distress.

#### 1.7 "Julian" (vd_corner_content: Tension=-1, Valence=+1)
**The Performance:** Julian is very similar to Silas, but there is a subtly different quality to the lower face. Because Tension is low, the jaw is firmly (but not tightly) closed. The lack of jaw-drop reinforces the "Calm" aspect. It feels more "composed" than Silas.
**The Story:** Julian is a master of Zen. His contentment is disciplined and internal. He is the anchor in the storm. The closed jaw gives him a sense of dignity and self-possession that the more "loose" Silas lacked.

#### 1.8 "Mordecai" (vd_corner_depressed: Tension=-1, Valence=-1)
**The Performance:** This is the most significant shift. Mordecai's jaw is closed. His mouth is a tight, downward-curving crescent. Unlike the slack-jawed Barnaby, Mordecai's face is a fortress of misery. His eyes are narrowed slightly, focusing the pain inward.
**The Story:** Mordecai is the stoic mourner. His grief is not a surrender; it is a burden he is carrying with grim determination. This is the "Depressed" face of a protagonist—someone who is crushed but still "there," still holding their shape. It is a much more powerful narrative image than the "melted" face of Barnaby.

## 2. Variant C vs D: Side-by-Side Confrontation

When we place these variants side-by-side, the systemic advantages of Variant D begin to emerge.

### Manic Quadrant: Felix (C) vs. Zippy (D)
*   **Readability:** Zippy (D) wins on readability. At a distance, the open jaw is a massive signal of high energy. Felix (C) could almost be mistaken for "Content" if you didn't see the eyes.
*   **Story:** Felix is "Fake Happy." Zippy is "Uncontrollably Happy." 
*   **Recommendation:** For a system meant to reflect raw internal state, Zippy is the superior representation of "Manic."

### Panicked Quadrant: Arthur (C) vs. Victor (D)
*   **Readability:** Victor (D) is the clear winner. The open jaw is the universal signifier of the "Gasp." Arthur (C) looks like he's trying to remember if he locked his car.
*   **Thumbnail Test:** At 120px, Victor's open mouth is a dark pit that clearly signals "Extreme State." Arthur's mouth disappears into a single line.

### Content Quadrant: Silas (C) vs. Julian (D)
*   **Readability:** Both are excellent. The differences are subtle.
*   **Richness:** Silas (C) feels a bit more "real" in a casual sense—a slightly loose jaw is natural for a relaxed person. Julian (D) feels a bit more "rendered" or "posed." 
*   **Verdict:** Variant C's "Content" is slightly more evocative of true relaxation, but only by a hair.

### Depressed Quadrant: Barnaby (C) vs. Mordecai (D)
*   **Readability:** This is a philosophical choice. Barnaby (C) reads as "Exhaustion/Defeat." Mordecai (D) reads as "Deep Grief/Grimness."
*   **The Story:** Mordecai is a much more compelling character. Barnaby looks like a victim; Mordecai looks like a survivor. For performance capture in a narrative context, Mordecai provides a much more solid foundation for an actor to build upon.

## 3. Quadrant Distinctiveness: The Search for a Clear Language

A successful facial visualization system must ensure that the four corners of the map are as far apart as possible, not just mathematically, but visually.

### Variant C's Set:
If you put the 4 vc_ faces in a row, the primary differentiator is the mouth corner (Valence) and the eyes (Tension). However, the jaw feels somewhat inconsistent. Why does "Depressed" have an open mouth while "Manic" has a closed one? This creates a confusing visual grammar where "Open Mouth" can mean "Sadness" OR "Extreme Surprised Joy" (if the eyes were different).

### Variant D's Set:
In the vd_ set, the visual grammar is iron-clad:
- **Upper Half (Tension +1):** Open Jaw. "High Volume."
- **Lower Half (Tension -1):** Closed Jaw. "Low Volume."
- **Right Half (Valence +1):** Upturned Corners. "Positive."
- **Left Half (Valence -1):** Downturned Corners. "Negative."

This creates a 4-way differentiation that is incredibly robust. You can identify the quadrant of a Variant D face even if the image is blurry or small, simply by looking at the "Silhouette of the Mouth." The "V-shape" of Manic, the "Square-shape" of Panicked, the "Line-shape" of Content, and the "Inverted-U-shape" of Depressed are distinct geometric primitives.

## 4. The Jaw Question: Philosophical and Visual Metaphors

The core of this comparison is the jaw. Is "Jaw = Urgency" a better visual metaphor than "Jaw = Valence"?

As a cinematographer, I argue that **Jaw = Urgency** is the more "truthful" mapping for a performance capture system. In the human animal, the jaw drops for two primary reasons: to vocalize (scream, shout, gasp) or because of total muscular failure (extreme exhaustion, death).

By linking the jaw to Valence (Variant C), we were prioritizing the "muscular failure" aspect of sadness. This is valid, but it is a "passive" state. 

By linking the jaw to Tension (Variant D), we are prioritizing the "vocalization/arousal" aspect. This is an "active" state. In most interactive or narrative systems, we are more interested in the *active* expressions of the characters. We want to see them scream in fear, shout in joy, or grit their teeth in quiet determination. 

**The "Internalization" Effect:**
One of the most beautiful side-effects of Variant D is what it does to the "Calm" faces. By forcing the jaw closed in low-tension states, it makes the character feel more "internal." Their emotions are happening *inside* them, behind a composed mask. This creates a wonderful contrast when they finally "break" into a high-tension state and the jaw drops—it feels like a genuine release of pressure.

## 5. Recommendations: Refining the Performance

While Variant D is the clear winner for its clarity and narrative power, I have a few suggestions to refine the recipe:

1.  **The "Mordecai" Brow:** In `vd_corner_depressed.png`, the brows are a bit neutral. To really sell the "Deep Grief" without the open jaw, we might want to increase the "inner brow raise" (corrugator interaction) just a touch more in the negative valence/negative tension quadrant. This will ensure he doesn't just look "Grumpy."
2.  **The Jaw Curve:** The transition from closed to open jaw along the Tension axis should be non-linear. It should stay relatively closed until Tension hits +0.5, then "pop" open more rapidly. This will preserve the "Internal" feel of the neutral/low states while still giving us the "Explosion" of the manic/panicked states.
3.  **Eye-Jaw Coupling:** We should consider a subtle "auto-wide" for the eyes when the jaw is open at Tension=+1. Zippy and Victor already have wide eyes, but ensuring this coupling is baked into the recipe will prevent "Dead Eye/Screaming Mouth" syndrome.
4.  **Skin Tone and Tension:** In the Panicked (Victor) quadrant, we should consider a slight "paling" or "graying" of the skin tone to simulate the vasoconstriction of terror. In the Manic (Zippy) quadrant, a slight "flush" or "redness" would sell the high-blood-pressure ecstasy.
