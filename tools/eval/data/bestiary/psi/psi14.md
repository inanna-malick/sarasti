## Description

Psi14 is the left-sided member of the second asymmetric conjugate pair (psi14/psi15). Where the psi11/psi12 pair lateralizes mouth corner movement, this pair lateralizes upper face / brow movement. The architecture is the same: one component handles the left hemisphere, its conjugate handles the right, and together they produce bilateral expression.

At positive values, the left brow lifts subtly and the left periorbital region widens. The right side of the upper face remains comparatively neutral. The overall impression is a very mild one-sided skepticism or quizzical regard — a single raised eyebrow quality, though much gentler than a true brow raise.

At negative values, the left brow area depresses and the left eye region narrows marginally. A subtle left-sided furrowing.

## Conjugate Pair: psi14 + psi15

Following the same pattern as psi11/psi12, pairing psi14 with psi15 at equal positive weights would produce a bilateral upper-face expression — both brows lifting, both periorbital regions widening. This would be a different character of brow expression than psi2 (which is a symmetric primary component with much larger deformation amplitude and more whole-face involvement).

The conjugate pair architecture documented in CLAUDE.md lists psi14/psi15 as a known pair, consistent with what the renders show.

## Symmetry

Asymmetric (score ~0.4). Left-lateralized by design. The 3/4 views confirm differential left/right deformation.

## Practical Notes

- Not currently used in any expression recipe.
- Potential use: adding asymmetric upper-face character — a one-sided skeptical quality could enhance the "wired/suspicious" pole of the fatigue axis.
- Paired with psi15: bilateral brow expression for nuanced alertness or surprise layering.
- Very subtle at tested weights. Would need significant weights (3-4x) to be visible alongside primary component deformations.
- No mesh artifacts.

## Confidence

Low-medium. The asymmetric character is consistent with the documented conjugate pair pattern, lending structural confidence even where the per-pixel visual changes are subtle.
